Let's talk about Financial independence
When the cash flow from your investments exceeds your expenses
This diagram is (obviously) highly simplified but outlines the basic math of FI. This is pretty powerful as you know the 2 levers to manipulate to get to it.
Also once you’re on a path, you can compute it.
The 2 levers of FI:
  • Increase cash flow
  • Decrease expenses
  • Increase cash flow: increase how much you stash (saving rate, see below) or the quality of your investment (returns)
  • Decrease your expenses
Increase cash flow

Decrease expenses

It turns out that they are not equal!

$1 expense = $25 of capital for cash flow
Using the 4% rule.
If you get better returns, the amount of capital will be lower. However I don’t know of any investments returning 100% per year forever so the multiplier is always > 1x.
$1 of capital for cash flow = $1.5 - $2 in income
That’s to account for taxes.
For high-income earners (which I hope is most folks reading this), it’s closer to $2. $2 is a good rule of thumb.
$1 expense = $38 - $50 in income
This is the capital T Truth about expenses. The returns are pretty low with a diversified portfolio so your expenses do matter.
\[\begin{aligned} saving\_rate & = \frac{income - expenses}{income} \end{aligned} \]
Very powerful concept: the saving rate is how much you’re stashing away.
The closer you get to 100% the faster you will reach FI (exponentially so).
Saving rate (%) Years to FI

Note that the relationship is not linear. The closer you get to 100% the faster it goes.
That's the power of a high saving rate!
