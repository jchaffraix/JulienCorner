The 2 levers of FI:
  • Increase cash flow
  • Decrease expenses
  • Increase cash flow: increase how much you stash (saving rate, see below) or the quality of your investment (returns)
  • Decrease your expenses
  • Increase cash flow

  • Decrease expenses

It turns out that they are not equal!

Who has heard about the 4% rule?
Ask someone to explain
Can take about 4% of the portfolio each year to last for the duration (adjusted for inflation).
Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR)
Hard question: stocks have highly variable returns
Hard to know how much to take each year
Study based on 20 years' periods
Rule of thumb for index fund
Stocks have highly variable returns
Lots of arguments for/against it (20 years assumptions, flexibility how much to get, risks depending on the time periods, ...)
Limited but useful
Index funds is only one asset class
index funds are easy and provide reasonable return
But they are volative (sequence of risks) and not the highest return (more active investments like real-estate, businesses can compound at 20%).
Better returns means less capital needed
This just changes the ratio of income to capital needed, not the math
However I don’t know of any investments returning 100% per year forever so the multiplier is always > 1x. For 10% returns, it's still 10x
$1 expense ⇌ $25 of capital invested for cash flow
This is the 4% rule, translated in $$
$1 of capital invested for cash flow ⇌ $1.5 - $2 in income
That’s to account for taxes.
For high-income earners, this is closer to $2. $2 is a good rule of thumb.
$1 expense ⇌ $37.5 - $50 in income
This is the capital T Truth about expenses. The returns are pretty low with a diversified portfolio so your expenses do matter.

\[\begin{aligned} saving\_rate & = \frac{income - expenses}{income} \end{aligned} \]
Very powerful concept: the saving rate is how much you’re stashing away.
The closer you get to 100% the faster you will reach FI (exponentially so).

Adopted from Mister Money Moustache
Note that the relationship is not linear. The closer you get to 100% the faster it goes.
That's the power of a high saving rate!
